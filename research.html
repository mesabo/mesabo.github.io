Research

I work at the intersection of power-system control theory, deep learning (graph neural networks, small large language models  —  sLM LLM), IoT systems, and grid cybersecurity. Most of my output focuses on time-series problems on energy and IoT data: forecasting, imputation, and anomaly detection. Validation scales from textbook benchmarks to real PMU field data, with all training and inference run on dedicated multi-GPU servers I configure and maintain end-to-end.

Recent research  —  Smart-grid co-optimization with LLM-driven anomaly detection

A unified co-optimization framework for smart-grid dispatch and communication routing with formal stability guarantees and small-language-model (sLM) based SCADA anomaly detection. The framework treats the grid as an IoT-instrumented system: PMU streams, edge gateways, and SCADA endpoints are first-class data sources. All training and inference run on a dedicated multi-GPU server in the YuLab. This is the active doctoral project (2025 onwards).

Three pillars

  1. Formal delay-stability coupling bounds. Closed-form stability bounds where delay-margin coupling is learned per generator from operational data.

Method & expected validation

Built on a dual-domain graph neural network operating jointly in the spectral domain (Laplacian eigenstructure of the grid) and the temporal domain (PMU time-series), producing per-generator coupling parameters whose physical interpretability is preserved by an additive decomposition.

Expected validation targets. Stability margin error within 5% of analytical baselines on IEEE 14, 30, 57, 118-bus benchmarks; runtime under 200 ms per evaluation on a single workstation GPU.

  1. Multi-impairment stability analysis. Generalises the delay-only bound to compound impairments (delay packet loss jitter), each as a physically interpretable additive component.

Method & expected validation

A margin-erosion decomposition maps each impairment to an additive component with learned per-generator weights, enabling per-component sensitivity analysis without losing physical interpretability.

Expected validation targets. Bound tightness under +/-20% impairment perturbation across 8 power-system benchmarks up to PEGASE 2,869-bus; full reproducibility on public OpenDSS and pandapower test cases.

  1. SLM-based SCADA anomaly prediction. Predicts impairment trajectories from SCADA / PMU streams to trigger preventive dispatch before stability margins are breached.

Method & expected validation

Built on the spectrally-reprogrammed small-language-model (sLM) pipeline of Spec2LLM and LLM4Imp, operating on tokenised PMU windows from IoT-edge gateways. A control loop couples the prediction to the dispatch optimiser of pillar 1. Inference is colocated on the same dedicated GPU server as training, with the aim of keeping latency under the 30 s leading-time target.

Expected validation targets. Anomaly leading-time of at least 30 s before the empirical instability threshold on real PMU field data, with a false-alarm rate under 5%.

Secondary track  —  Spectral reprogramming of language models for time-series

A frozen language model  —  sLM or LLM  —  reprogrammed with frequency-domain prompts, generalises across forecasting, imputation and anomaly tasks on energy and IoT time-series without per-task fine-tuning. Training runs on dedicated GPU servers; the sLM variants are explicitly designed so inference can be deployed close to IoT-edge gateways.

How the pipeline evolved

Spec2Llama introduced the spectral-aware forecasting frame: time-series tokens are projected into a learned frequency representation that prompts a frozen Llama backbone, enabling versatile time-series analysis across heterogeneous datasets without per-task fine-tuning.

Spec2LLM applied the same idea to power-transformer temperature forecasting, with a tighter spectral-to-language token coupling and validation on grid-equipment monitoring data.

LLM4Imp extended the pipeline to time-series imputation under heavy missing-data regimes (long gaps, irregular sampling) by reformulating imputation as a frequency-domain prompt completion task.

This pipeline is the foundation for pillar 3 of the doctoral project (SLM-based SCADA anomaly prediction).
